Problem Statement

International students and newcomers face barriers in understanding Canadian financial systems. Existing tools are either too technical or lack cultural/contextual relevance. How might we make financial literacy accessible, engaging, and trustworthy for these users?

Ideation

Brainstorming

Sketched gamified approaches (animal personalities, progress paths).

User Flows

Designed onboarding survey → personalized persona → course module flow.

Wireframes

Helped to visualize how a newcomer might discover, engage with, and apply lessons.

Prototyping

Low-Fidelity

Wireframes in Figma showing module cards, quizzes, and progress indicators.

High-Fidelity

Applied brand identity with purple/green/teal palette, playful icons, and accessible typography (sans-serif, large headings).

Design System

Used Figma Alignments, Spacing and UI Assets for consistency.

Usability Testing & Outcomes

Testing

5 Participants: international students

  • Completing a quiz
  • Checking progress
  • Reading module content

Findings

  • Progress bar and quiz feedback were motivating.
  • Users appreciated plain language + icons.
  • Navigation needed simplification (added breadcrumbs + clearer CTAs).
Outcome: Improved flow, increased satisfaction, validated gamified approach.

Reflect & Lessons Learned

  1. 1

    Simplicity Wins

    Designing financial education taught me that simplicity beats complexity. Users valued clarity and playfulness over feature overload.

  2. 2

    Accessibility Matters

    Applying contrast, alt text, and screen-reader labels made the tool more inclusive and easier to navigate.

  3. 3

    End-to-End Ownership

    Leading design from research → testing → dev handoff built confidence in managing full UX cycles.

Purple / Orange / Teal for a balance of trust + playfulness.

Accessibility
